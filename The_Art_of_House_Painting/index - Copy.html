The art of house painting
Artist is moved by the affection people have for their homes. 'It's very nice to produce something for someone who will treasure it. What I've noticed is that people truly love their houses and they create beautiful gardens around them'
 
STEPHANIE WHITTAKER
Freelance

Saturday, May 10, 2003
One of Varga's watercolour paintings: a home on Stratford Rd. in Hampstead. (Painting courtesy of J. Varga.)
 
ADVERTISEMENT

Every year, János Varga spends four months during the summer and autumn as a house painter. Not that he paints houses, mind you. He paints pictures of houses, commissioned by homeowners who love their digs and want to capture them in watercolours.

When he's not tackling his other jobs, as a computer teacher at John Abbott College during the winter and plant-

ing trees in northern Ontario during the late spring, Varga totes his easel and paints around to Montreal neighbourhoods and offers homeowners his artistic services.

A charming man who has lived a peripatetic existence in Australia, South Africa and various countries in Asia and Europe, Varga took up painting without formal training a decade ago.

"My grandfather was an artist, and I suppose it's in the genes," he said. "I had seen a calendar when I lived in South Africa. It had paintings of old houses in Durban and I thought to myself, 'I could do that.' Except I didn't know how to start."

Necessity provided the impetus in 1992, while Varga was living in Australia. "I couldn't find a job and I got some paper and pencils and sat down to draw a picture of a house," he recalled. "I offered the picture to the homeowner for $5, but he didn't want it. I did the same at a second house, and the owner said: 'No thanks. I already know what my house looks like.' The third homeowner told me he already had a portrait of his house. But when I went to the fourth house, I had three pictures I could show the homeowner and he liked what he saw and asked me to do a pen-and-ink drawing."

Whenever he has pulled up stakes and moved to another country, Varga has taken his growing artistic skill with him. In Victoria, B.C., one man commissioned him to paint a portrait of a house that had not yet been built. "He took me to a vacant lot where he was to have his house built," Varga said. "He said he was intimidated by the architect he'd hired and sat beside me while I drew - with his instructions - the kind of house he wanted the architect to design. Then he took the drawing to the architect."

Usually, however, people hire Varga for more sentimental reasons. "I remember one couple who were so in love, so happy," he said. "And they loved their house. They showed me the chairs they sat on to have a beer every evening after work. They wanted to be able to remember that experience and they had me paint that view.

"Another homeowner, in Toronto, had planted trees to mark the birth of each of her children and wanted to make sure the trees were all in the painting."

Varga said one woman hired him to paint a picture of her parents' home while they were away on vacation as a surprise. "She showed me the art they had hanging in their house and I saw that they favoured moody watercolours. So I did a similar moody painting of their house."

He said some homeowners ask for a misty watercolour rendering while others want crisp lines. "If they ask for crispness, I use pen and ink with the watercolour to create harder lines," he said.

Other Toronto homeowners asked Varga to create a collage of various beloved elements - a Japanese bird feeder, a statue of a frog in the garden and two wooden sculptures of cranes. "She also wanted a view of the garden across her dinner table," he said.

Real-estate agents engage him to paint portraits of the homes they sell to give their clients as housewarming gifts.

Varga clearly enjoys his work and is moved by the love people have for their homes. "It's very nice to produce something for someone who will treasure it. What I've noticed is that people truly love their houses and they create beautiful gardens around them. I did a painting for one elderly couple who had built a garden patio and they wanted to remember all the parties they'd had on it.

"Other people invest in their children. One homeowner asked me to paint in her children's toys, which were scattered throughout the front yard."

Varga often has a pied-piper effect on children in the neighbourhoods in which he paints. "Sometimes, I'll sit at my easel in front of a house and kids will bring their own paper and paint and sit with me while they do their own painting," he said.

János Varga's work can be seen at www.eyeswideweb.com/janos/gallery
